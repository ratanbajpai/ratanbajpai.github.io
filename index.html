Relation Between Per Capita GDP and Working Hours in OECD Countries

This presentation attempts to find any correlation between the number of annual hours worked per worker and the per capita GDP in OECD countries. The Organiation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) is an inter-governmental organisation formed in 1961. It's purpose is to stimulate economic progress and world trade. Although the countries listed in this dataset are spread out across all the continents, there is very little representation from Africa and the Middle Eastern region. In terms of economic prosperity, the data is well represented by rich and poor countries. This visualisation can attempt to answer questions like: "Do people in rich countries work longer hours?"

Data Set

The source for data used in this visualisation is from: Groningen Growth and Development Center. This is a large dataset with several fields for various economic and developmental indicators. The total dataset spans almost last 70 years. However, data related to number of annual hours worked is somewhat limited. This specific information is not available for a group of countries prior to 1990s. The latest total data available is till the year 2019.

Visualization

The data in this visualisation is presented as interactive bubble plot, with per capita GDP on X-axis, whereas the average annual hours per worker on Y-axis. The size of the bubbles is proportional to the population of the countries shown. The per capita GDP is represented in 2017 US$. The population information is rounded to the nearest million. The data is depicted as a slide show, where each slide shows data for a particular year. The three years chosen are almost 20 years apart each. This choice clearly shows how the working hours and per capita GDP has changed for several countries. More information is available when the cursor is moved to a specific country.



Observations

The trend that can be noticed from this visualisation is that as decades have passed, the per capita GDP has obviously increased, but the number of annual working hours per worker has declined for almost all of the countries barring a few. This can be attributed to the fact that industrialization and automation have increased worker productivity. It can also be noticed that for developed economies (higher per capita GDP) in Europe and North America, the working hours are markedly less than the poorer and developing economies of the Asian countries. There are a couple exceptions to this as in the case of Singapore. Even though it has high per capita GDP, it's workers on average tend to work longer hours. Another interesting observation is that in the last two decades, Ireland has been able to grow it's per capita GDP substantially while also bringing down the number of hours worked by it's workers.

Conclusions

From this visualization, it's easy to see that economic prosperity across the world is greatly unequal. In highly developed nations in Europe and North America, people tend to work a lot less hours with respect to some of the poorer nations in the Asian subcontinent. People in Ireland or Switzerland which are one of the wealthiest nations today, have an average income that is more than 20 times than that of people in Cambodia or Myanmar. However, an average worker in Cambodia puts in 900 more hours a year than an average worker in Switzerland. This can be attributed to the fact that these developed nations have been able to leverage technology and automation to drive up productivity and per capita GDP substantially. It's quite clear from the data that workers in poorer countries work much more in some cases than their rich counterparts.
